## Major themes and subthemes

- Regional transferability and climate fit
  - Mid-Atlantic realism
  - Eastern U.S. applicability
  - season length, overwintering window, spring termination timing
- Cover crops, biomass, and nitrogen
  - hairy vetch + cereal rye mixtures
  - biomass vs N tradeoffs
  - seeding proportion
  - soil texture, previous crop, GDD effects
- Reduced tillage and weed suppression
  - cover crop-based organic rotational no-till
  - roller-crimper systems
  - cereal rye mulch performance
  - weed-management constraints in humid Eastern systems
- Rotation design and economics
  - long-term organic vs conventional performance
  - price-premium dependence
  - risk and return by rotation length
  - hay in rotation
- Sustainability framing
  - environmental harms of industrial agriculture
  - health, pollution, biodiversity, soil depletion
  - broad rationale, not Mid-Atlantic practice detail

- "What cover crop mix should I use for biomass, N, or mulch?"
  - Start: `Thapa-et-al-2018-hairy-vetch-cereal-rye-meta-analysis.pdf`

- "How much hairy vetch is enough in a vetch/rye mix?"
  - Start: `Thapa-et-al-2018-hairy-vetch-cereal-rye-meta-analysis.pdf`
  - Key cue: mixture N matched or exceeded the best monoculture when hairy vetch seed proportion exceeded about 46% by weight.

## Unresolved gaps and weak evidence areas

- Direct Mid-Atlantic vegetable-crop timing guidance is weak in this folder; most direct evidence here is field-crop oriented.
- Disease-pressure guidance is mostly implicit in the expert brief; the local paper set does not give strong disease-management detail.
- The Mirsky local `.md` note is thin; real detail depends on following the DOI-linked paper.
- The Horrigan review is valuable for rationale but weak for local operational recommendations.
- The Thapa meta-analysis is strong for mixture behavior across studies, but not a full local calendar or cultivar-specific prescription for one exact site.
- Economics evidence is strongest for grain/forage rotations, not small-scale diversified market-garden systems.
