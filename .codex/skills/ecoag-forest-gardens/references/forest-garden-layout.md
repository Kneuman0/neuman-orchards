# Forest Garden Layout Reference

This reference is for layout reasoning, not just layer definitions. Use it when the question is about how a forest garden should be arranged so that light, water, roots, access, and time all work together.

## What A Forest Garden Is Structurally

Forest gardens are not just "many plants together." Structurally, they behave like deliberately managed early-successional or edge-like perennial systems rather than closed, mature forest. The core pattern is:

- a widely spaced overstory that does not fully close the canopy
- multiple vertical layers occupying different light niches
- deliberate overlap between edible production, habitat, nutrient cycling, and microclimate regulation
- increasing shade, root competition, litter production, and self-selection as the system matures

The local literature is consistent on a few layout consequences:

- Temperate forest gardens are typically described as multi-layered or multistrata perennial systems designed to mimic forest or woodland structure while remaining harvestable and human-directed (`Castro-et-al-2018-temperate-agroforestry-forest-gardens.pdf`, pp. 2, 7; `Castro-et-al-2025-temperate-food-forests-scoping-review.pdf`, pp. 2-3).
- Their ecological behavior depends on heterogeneity: varied canopy heights, different rooting depths, patchy shade, and mixed species create microclimate buffering, biodiversity gains, and multiple niches (`Jose-2019-agroforestry-and-biodiversity.pdf`, pp. 1, 11-15).
- Structural complexity improves function only when competition is managed. The same local literature that praises multistrata systems also notes light competition, belowground competition, and management difficulty as real constraints (`Castro-et-al-2025-temperate-food-forests-scoping-review.pdf`, p. 8; `Bishop-et-al-2023-eastern-black-walnut-alley-cropping-review.pdf`, pp. 4-6).

The practical implication is that layout should be treated as controlled partial canopy, not full canopy.

## Major Vertical Layers

The local folder strongly supports the idea of canopy, understory, shrub, herbaceous, root, and climber layers, but it is thin on a standardized design breakdown. For that layer-by-layer applied framing, this file also uses recent ATTRA and Extension guidance.[^attra-forest-garden] [^umn-forest-farming]

| Layer | Structural role | Typical light niche | Common layout techniques | Common plant functions |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Canopy / overstory | Sets the upper light ceiling, wind filtering, litter regime, and long-term root competition | Full sun; casts part to deep shade below | Use widest spacing; favor north-side placement when possible; prune and thin to prevent total closure; reserve for the few species that justify the space cost | Staple fruit/nut, timber, shade, microclimate buffering, wildlife structure |
| Subcanopy / understory trees | Fills the mid-height harvest zone and converts filtered light into production | Full sun to bright part shade depending on overstory openness | Place in canopy gaps, south/east edges of taller trees, or between larger trees during early years with future thinning in mind | Easier harvest fruit, small nuts, flowers, pollinator support |
| Shrub layer | Creates medium-density production and edge habitat; often the most flexible woody layer | Sun to part shade; some species tolerate bright shade | Use as patch-forming groups, path edges, hedges, gap fillers, and transitions between tree zones and herb layer | Berries, habitat, nectar, chop-and-drop biomass, screening |
| Herbaceous layer | Occupies the most dynamic zone for nutrient cycling, pollinator support, mulch generation, and short-rotation yields | Full sun to shade depending on species and canopy age | Use in drifts or guild rings rather than isolated singles; bias high-management crops close to paths | Culinary herbs, perennial vegetables, dynamic accumulators, insectary plants, medicinals |
| Groundcover layer | Protects soil, reduces weed pressure, moderates evaporation, and stabilizes the understory floor | Part sun to shade; some species tolerate sun if moisture is adequate | Use as living mulch under shrubs and trees, but keep trunks and crowns clear enough to reduce disease pressure | Soil cover, edible berries/leaves, pollinator forage, erosion control |
| Root / rhizosphere layer | Uses belowground space not filled by major woody roots; often shares space with herbaceous plants | Driven more by soil depth, drainage, and root competition than by aboveground light alone | Reserve for places with workable soil depth and manageable woody competition; avoid dense planting right at major tree root flare zones | Tubers, bulbs, medicinal roots, nutrient storage, soil opening |
| Vine / climber layer | Converts existing vertical structure into extra yield without dedicating more ground area | Sun to part shade depending on support and species | Use on trellises, fences, arbors, or selected trees/shrubs only when load and shading are acceptable | Fruit, beans, habitat, seasonal shade |
| Fungi layer | Converts woody mulch or logs in cool, moist shade into food and decomposition services | Shade to deep shade with stable moisture | Use in log yards, mulched pockets, north sides of plantings, and irrigation-adjacent shade zones | Culinary mushrooms, decomposition, soil-food-web support |

## How The Structure Behaves Over Time

- Year 0-3: Light is abundant and weeds are aggressive. This is the best window for dense herbaceous filling, short-lived support species, and rapid soil cover.
- Year 3-8: Shrub and subcanopy competition starts to matter. This is the critical correction window for thinning, pruning, path retention, and deciding which nurse plants stay.
- Year 8+: Shade and root competition intensify. Productivity shifts away from sun-demanding understory species unless canopy openness is actively maintained.

Local evidence supports this dynamic view. Temperate food-forest literature is stronger on establishment and framing than on mature-system management, and it explicitly flags long establishment times plus the need to manage competition and spacing (`Castro-et-al-2025-temperate-food-forests-scoping-review.pdf`, p. 8). The layout lesson is to plan for removal, not just planting.

## Layer-By-Layer Layout Reasoning

### 1. Canopy Layer

Use the canopy sparingly. Every canopy tree commits the site to decades of shade pattern, litter chemistry, root pressure, and harvest logistics.

Common techniques:

- keep canopy tree count lower than beginners expect
- leave crown-development room beyond nursery spacing assumptions
- favor open-canopy, later-leafing, or more prunable trees where understory production matters
- place the tallest trees so they do not erase southern light access to the rest of the system
- use alleys, gaps, or broad crescents rather than uniform closure

Relevant local mechanisms:

- tree canopy regulates the lower-layer microclimate (`Ortiz-et-al-2020-forests-agroforestry-giahs-review.pdf`, pp. 12, 15)
- shade can help lower layers in heat and drought, but excessive shade reduces understory yield and increases competition (`Bishop-et-al-2023-eastern-black-walnut-alley-cropping-review.pdf`, pp. 4-6)

### 2. Subcanopy Layer

This is usually the highest-value harvest zone for human-scale food forests because it is easier to access than the canopy and can exploit filtered light.

Common techniques:

- place on canopy edges and in seasonal sun lanes
- use staggered placement instead of direct stacking under dense canopy
- plan for future thinning if planted as temporary fillers in young systems
- group by harvest timing and pruning style, not just by species

### 3. Shrub Layer

Shrubs are the main "bridge" layer. They turn unused side light into yield, stabilize edges, and create beneficial habitat without the same long-term lock-in as canopy trees.

Common techniques:

- thicken sunny edges and path margins with shrubs
- use shade-tolerant shrubs in bright understory pockets, not in the darkest core
- create patches rather than evenly spaced dots
- leave harvest corridors and air movement between dense berry blocks

Local support:

- multi-species tree and shrub combinations are repeatedly associated with stronger biodiversity and habitat outcomes than simple woody structure (`Jose-2019-agroforestry-and-biodiversity.pdf`, pp. 3, 8-9, 15)

### 4. Herbaceous Layer

This is where layout becomes management-sensitive. A forest garden can look full on paper but fail if the herb layer is placed without regard to changing shade, foot traffic, and mulch access.

Common techniques:

- cluster high-touch species near paths, entrances, and water access
- use shade-tolerant herbs where canopy closure is expected
- put aggressive biomass plants where they can be cut repeatedly without blocking harvest
- match herb height and seasonal timing to the layer above

### 5. Groundcover Layer

Groundcovers are for soil control first, yield second. They should suppress unwanted vegetation and regulate moisture without choking crowns, paths, or harvest zones.

Common techniques:

- use as living mulch under shrubs and between widely spaced perennials
- keep a low-competition ring around young trunks
- prefer spreaders that are manageable under your maintenance level
- use denser groundcovers on slopes and exposed soil

### 6. Root Layer

Root crops belong where soil depth, texture, and woody competition allow digging and swelling. They are usually a poor fit directly adjacent to major woody roots.

Common techniques:

- place in looser soils, path shoulders, bed edges, or younger tree zones
- avoid pairing with dense shallow-rooted woody species
- use bulbs and narrow-rooted species in tighter niches; use tubers where expansion room exists

### 7. Vine Layer

Vines can be efficient or destructive. They work best when their support structure, pruning burden, and shade effect are planned first.

Common techniques:

- prefer independent trellises near paths for harvestable vines
- only run vines into trees where the host can tolerate the load and shading
- keep vines off slow-growing or high-value young trees
- use the vine layer to convert fence lines, pergolas, and sunny edges into production rather than darkening the system core

### 8. Fungi Layer

Fungi belong in the coolest and most moisture-stable parts of the design, often on the north side of woody structure, under mulch, or in designated log clusters.

Common techniques:

- place near irrigation or moisture catchments
- keep separate from the most trampled harvest lanes
- treat fungi as a shade-use strategy for spaces that are already too dim for most crops

## Plant Placement Matrix By Light And Site Trait

Use this table to categorize candidate plants before choosing exact species.

| Site condition | Best-fit layers | Plant traits to prioritize | Layout implication |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Full sun, hot exposure | Canopy, subcanopy, shrub, sun-tolerant herb and groundcover | Heat tolerance, drought tolerance, strong fruiting under high light | Put your most light-hungry crops here; do not waste these pockets on deep-shade species |
| Part sun / moving shade | Subcanopy, shrub, herbaceous, vine | Flexibility under changing light, moderate vigor, good response to pruning-managed canopy | This is the default productive band in many temperate forest gardens |
| Bright shade / dappled shade | Shrub, herbaceous, groundcover, root, fungi | Shade tolerance, broad leaves, early-season growth, moisture efficiency | Good for berries, herbs, roots, mushrooms, and support species rather than heavy-fruiting sun crops |
| Deep shade | Groundcover, fungi, selected roots and very shade-tolerant herbs | Shade-obligate or shade-forgiving growth, low yield expectation, low maintenance value | Use for access, mulch, fungi, or habitat if edible return is low |
| Dry slope / thin soil | Shrub, herbaceous, sparse groundcover, selected canopy only | Drought tolerance, strong anchoring roots, low fertility demand | Reduce density and avoid thirsty stacking; favor soil-covering and erosion-buffering plants |
| Moist but well-drained loam | Almost all layers | High productivity, broad species palette, good root penetration | This is where full layering is easiest |
| Wet pocket / poor drainage | Selected canopy, shrub, groundcover; limited roots | Flood tolerance, disease tolerance, oxygen-poor soil tolerance | Do not force standard forest-garden layers into chronically wet soil; simplify and use adapted species |
| Frost pocket / cold-air sink | Groundcover, shrub, hardy subcanopy; avoid sensitive early bloomers | Late bloom, frost tolerance, low height where cold settles | Keep tender flowering tree crops off the lowest cold basin |
| Wind-exposed edge | Canopy, shrub, vine on strong support | Wind firmness, flexible stems, shelterbelt function, pollinator value | Build porous woody shelter first, then place sensitive crops behind it |
| High root competition near mature trees | Groundcover, fungi, selected herbs | Shallow or narrow rooting, shade tolerance, low nutrient demand | Treat as an understory niche, not a standard bed |
| High human traffic / frequent harvest | Herbaceous, shrub, vine, selected subcanopy | Easy harvest, repeat harvest, low snag risk, clear identity | Put labor-intensive and easily damaged crops near paths and entrances |

## Fast Heuristics For Actual Layout Decisions

- Put the tallest and widest trees where they preserve the most future light for everything else.
- Treat edge, gap, and dappled-shade zones as the highest-value production zones.
- Match deep-rooted woody plants with shallower-rooted companions to reduce direct belowground competition.[^attra-tips]
- Use shade-tolerant species in lower layers unless you are actively managing canopy openness through pruning and thinning.[^attra-forest-garden]
- Keep enough path width for pruning, mulching, harvest, and eventual replacement work.
- Expect to thin. A forest garden that cannot lose plants will usually become too dark.
- On slopes, use rows or planting lines that follow topography when water slowing and infiltration matter.[^attra-forest-garden] [^umn-alley]
- Where wind is strong, establish protective woody structure before expecting reliable performance from tender herb or vine layers.[^attra-tips]

## Common Failure Modes

- Too many canopy trees too close together
- placing sun crops into future shade without a thinning plan
- stacking plants by edible category instead of by compatible light and rooting niche
- no dedicated access lanes for pruning, mulch movement, and harvest
- overusing vigorous vines on young trees
- planting root crops into dense woody root zones
- treating every shaded space as productive instead of admitting some space should be mulch, access, habitat, or fungi

## What This Resource Can And Cannot Decide

This resource helps with structural placement logic, layer fit, and site-trait routing. It does not replace:

- species-level cultivar selection
- Mid-Atlantic climate filtering
- pest-disease compatibility screening
- soil fertility prescriptions
- yield forecasting
