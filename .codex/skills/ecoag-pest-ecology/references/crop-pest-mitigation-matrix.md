# Crop-Pest Mitigation Matrix

## Scope

This resource translates the local pest-ecology literature into a crop-group scouting and response matrix for the plants contemplated in this directory. It is grounded first in the local pest-ecology folder and the local plant inventories, then supplemented with extension/IPM sources only where the local folder is explicitly thin on crop-specific mapping.

Use it as a first-pass field guide:

1. Match the crop to a crop group.
2. Identify the dominant pest guild.
3. Read the likely system signal before reaching for a direct control.
4. Use the mitigation categories to tighten culture, canopy, timing, habitat, and exclusion.

This is not a spray-threshold guide. It is a local ecological response map.

## Coverage note

- Inventory examples come from the local perennial and self-seeding inventories, grouped by shared pest pressure and response logic rather than repeated plant-by-plant.
- The local pest folder is strong on ecological mechanisms, conservation biocontrol, nutrient-linked susceptibility, and habitat design, but weak on crop-specific home-garden protocols. `references/taxonomy.md` identifies that gap directly, which is why outside extension/IPM sources are used here for brassicas, alliums, solanaceous crops, fruits, berries, and vines.
- Nut trees, broad forest crops, and some aquatic/wetland species still need a later expansion if the skill is expected to answer those groups at the same detail level.

## Cross-cutting diagnostic matrix

| Pest guild or pattern | Likely system signal | First ecological move | Core local basis |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Aphids, whiteflies, leafhoppers, many other sap feeders | Excess soluble nitrogen, flushy weak tissue, water imbalance, weak predator pressure, tight canopy, heat-reflective stress | Slow nitrogen push, restore even moisture, open canopy, protect hoverflies/lacewings/parasitoids, use hard water spray before stronger interventions | `L2`, `L3`, `L7` |
| Flea beetles and similar early shot-hole feeders | Slow seedling establishment, cool or dry spring stress, exposed soil, pest carryover in residue or nearby host weeds | Accelerate establishment, cover at planting, remove debris, use kaolin or exclusion while plants are small | `L2`, `L3`, `E1`, `E2` |
| Brassica caterpillars and similar chewing larvae | Missed exclusion timing, continuous host bridge from weeds or succession crops, weak nectar support for parasitoids | Install row cover early, destroy residues, keep umbels plus calendula/chamomile/borage nectar nearby, hand-remove early larvae | `L3`, `L4`, `L5`, `E3` |
| Leafminers | Alternate hosts and weeds nearby, uncovered flight windows, infested residue or shallow soil carryover | Cover before egg-laying, remove mined leaves and host weeds, disrupt pupae only where history justifies it | `L2`, `L3`, `E5` |
| Slugs and snails | Cool wet mulch, surface shelter, dense canopy, weak morning sun, seedlings growing too slowly | Reduce shelters, water earlier, open airflow, trap and hand-remove, use iron phosphate only if pressure persists | `L3`, `L6`, `E7` |
| Spider mites and hot-season thrips | Heat, drought, dust, water stress, broad-spectrum spray history, predator disruption | Wash foliage, keep even moisture, cut dust, preserve predatory mites and minute pirate bugs | `L3`, `L4`, `E12` |
| Repeating outbreaks in simple beds | Floral gaps, low refuge, too much bare soil, too much tillage or cleanup, little alternative prey/pollen | Build season-long habitat bands, keep some low-disturbance cover, use layered bloom timing instead of one-off flower patches | `L1`, `L3`, `L4`, `L6`, `L8`, `L9` |

## Expanded mitigation matrix

### Brassicas and mustards

- Inventory coverage:
  Perennial kale / broccoli types, sea kale, Turkish rocket, perennial arugula, garden arugula, mizuna, tatsoi, bok choy, mustard greens, black mustard, turnip, radish, daikon, wild radish, nasturtium-as-support, and other brassica volunteers from `I1-I3`.
- Major pest types:
  Flea beetles, imported cabbageworm and related caterpillars, harlequin bug, cabbage/root maggots, slugs on young seedlings and in cool wet beds.
- Likely system signals:
  Flea beetles usually mean seedlings are exposed and slow, especially in hot dry spring weather. Caterpillar pressure often means row cover went on late or nearby brassica weeds and volunteers kept the host bridge open. Harlequin bug pressure often rises where winter debris and brassica residues stay in place. `L2`, `L3`, `E2`, `E3`, `E4`
- Cultural practices:
  Push for fast early growth with even moisture and steady fertility rather than soluble-nitrogen surges. Use transplants for high-value spring brassicas when direct-seeded stands get chewed too hard. Prefer short bed turnovers over leaving half-spent brassicas lingering as pest bridges. `L3`, `E1`, `E2`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Avoid tight late-season brassica canopies that trap humidity and hide caterpillars and harlequin bug nymphs. Thin mixed mustard beds early so leaves dry quickly in the morning. `L3`
- Companion and support plants:
  Keep small-flowered umbels and low nectar strips close enough to feed parasitoids and hoverflies without shading the crop. Good fits from the local inventories include dill, cilantro, parsley, fennel, chervil, calendula, chamomile, borage, and caraway for bloom continuity. Trap-crop use of mustard or nasturtium is possible, but only if the trap planting is monitored and cut back before it becomes a reservoir. `L3`, `L4`, `L5`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Use perennial edges, low-disturbance mulch bands, and adjacent flowering support to keep lacewings, hoverflies, parasitoids, ground beetles, and spiders active. Do not strip the whole bed area to bare soil between successions. `L4`, `L6`, `L8`, `L9`
- Timing:
  Install floating row cover at planting or transplanting, not after shot-holes appear. For fall brassicas, scout again for flea beetles and caterpillars instead of assuming the spring pattern is over. Favor fast-maturing cultivars where imported cabbageworm pressure is chronic. `E1`, `E2`, `E3`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Row cover is the main ecological tool here. Remove crop debris after harvest. Crush harlequin bug eggs and handpick caterpillars early. Kaolin clay can suppress flea-beetle feeding during establishment. `E1`, `E2`, `E3`, `E4`
- Other ecological responses:
  Bt is the best fit when caterpillars are still small and habitat repair is not enough. Use it as a targeted biological correction, not a replacement for exclusion and parasitoid support. `E3`

### Alliums

- Inventory coverage:
  Chives, garlic chives, Welsh onion, Egyptian walking onion, elephant garlic/perennial leek types, German garlic, meadow garlic, ramps, and other Allium spp. in `I1`.
- Major pest types:
  Allium leafminer, onion/root maggot, thrips, bulb mites, occasional slug pressure in cool mulch.
- Likely system signals:
  Repeated damage commonly tracks missed spring and fall flight windows for allium leafminer, not just "bad luck." Secondary rots often follow mining wounds. Thrips pressure tends to build in hot, dry, crowded foliage. `L2`, `L3`, `E5`, `E12`
- Cultural practices:
  Keep alliums on a clean rotation where possible and do not let infested foliage persist. Avoid overfertilizing with nitrogen once plants are established; soft sappy growth attracts sap-feeding pressure and can worsen secondary breakdowns. `L3`, `L7`, `E5`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Give bunching alliums enough spacing that leaves dry quickly and scouting lines remain visible. Dense clumps can hide oviposition rows and hold humidity around damaged tissue. `L3`
- Companion and support plants:
  Use nearby umbels plus chamomile, calendula, borage, mountain mint, and anise hyssop to support parasitoids and predators without shading the alliums. Avoid stuffing alliums into dense mixed beds where covers and scouting become impractical. `L3`, `L4`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Preserve nearby beneficial habitat, but keep host weeds out of the immediate allium zone. Leafminer guidance specifically calls out weed cleanup around the crop. `L4`, `E5`
- Timing:
  Cover spring alliums by late February before adults emerge. Cover fall plantings during the September flight. Delay spring planting until mid-May or delay fall planting into early November where ALM pressure is annual and the cropping system allows it. `E5`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Row cover or netting is the main ecological control. Remove allium debris after harvest. Do not cover an already-infested planting and trap the flies underneath. Discard rotted mined bulbs. `E5`
- Other ecological responses:
  If the system repeatedly fails on ALM, simplify the allium calendar instead of adding more sprays. A narrower spring/fall allium window is often the more ecological answer than fighting both adult flights every year. `L2`, `E5`

### Apiaceae herbs and roots

- Inventory coverage:
  Skirret, celery, parsley, dill, cilantro, chervil, lovage, sweet cicely, honewort/mitsuba, fennel, Florence fennel, caraway, anise, parsnip, wild carrot, and related umbels in `I1-I3`.
- Major pest types:
  Aphids, leafminers, occasional slug pressure on seedlings, and root/crown damage where seedlings stall in compact or crusted soil.
- Likely system signals:
  Dense volunteer stands and drought-stressed fine foliage often precede aphid and leafminer trouble. The same plants that support beneficial insects can become unmonitored reservoirs if they are left tangled and unmanaged. `L3`, `E5`, `E6`
- Cultural practices:
  Keep emergence and early growth steady with fine tilth, even moisture, and shallow weed control. Thin self-seeding strips early. Do not push excessive nitrogen into parsley/celery/fennel beds; lush tissue favors sap feeders. `L3`, `L7`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Maintain enough spacing for air and sight lines, especially in parsley/celery/fennel blocks. Let the support umbels flower, but not all in the same row as the crop canopy if it destroys airflow. `L3`
- Companion and support plants:
  Apiaceae crops are themselves support plants for hoverflies and parasitoids. Use some individuals as intentionally flowering edge plants while keeping the production row younger and cleaner. Pair with strips of chamomile, calendula, borage, and mountain mint rather than crowding the crop row. `L3`, `L4`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Because these crops feed beneficial adults well, the main habitat question is placement. Keep flowering umbels close enough for spillover but far enough that scouting and harvest remain practical. `L4`, `L8`, `L9`
- Timing:
  Cover susceptible seedling beds before leafminer flights if damage has been annual. Let only part of the stand bolt at once so the whole planting does not age into a pest reservoir. `E5`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Remove mined leaves, clean host weeds, and re-sow or cut back old bolting plants once the beneficial function has been served. Hand removal is usually enough at garden scale when done early. `E5`
- Other ecological responses:
  Swallowtail caterpillars on dill, fennel, or parsley are usually a tolerance question, not a control emergency. The crop can often share some foliage if there is enough biomass. `L3`

### Chenopods, beets, chards, spinach, and amaranths

- Inventory coverage:
  Good King Henry, sea beet, beet, Swiss chard, spinach, orache, lamb's quarters, magenta spreen, quinoa, grain amaranth, leaf amaranth, and related greens from `I1-I3`.
- Major pest types:
  Beet and spinach leafminers, flea beetles, aphids, slugs, and occasional late defoliators.
- Likely system signals:
  Heavy chenopod weed presence, especially lamb's quarters, can maintain leafminer pressure. Rich manure-driven growth can also produce soft aphid-prone tissue. Dense, shady beds favor slugs. `L3`, `E5`, `E6`, `E7`
- Cultural practices:
  Keep chenopod weeds down near crop beds while preserving chosen support plants elsewhere. Favor steady growth over rank growth. Re-seed or succession-sow rather than nursing ragged old spinach/chard blocks deep into pest season. `L3`, `E5`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Thin for airflow and easy inspection of undersides. Swiss chard and amaranth can become dense enough to hide mines and aphids unless regularly opened up. `L3`
- Companion and support plants:
  Use nearby umbels, mountain mint edges, calendula, chamomile, borage, and anise hyssop to pull in predators and parasitoids. Keep support flowers adjacent, not smothering the crop canopy. `L3`, `L4`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Ground-beetle and spider habitat helps with generalist pressure, but mulch cannot become a slug shelter right against seedling stems. Use low-disturbance edges more than wet buried stem zones. `L6`, `E7`
- Timing:
  Put row cover on before known leafminer egg-laying periods in high-pressure beds. Harvest spinach and beet greens young if leafminer pressure ramps as weather warms. `E1`, `E5`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Remove mined leaves, host weeds, and badly infested successions. Use row cover early. Trap slugs with boards or shelters and clear them daily if beds stay cool and damp. `E5`, `E7`
- Other ecological responses:
  A strong water spray is often enough for aphids on chard, beet, and orache if predator populations are present and nitrogen is not excessive. `E6`

### Tender mixed leafy greens

- Inventory coverage:
  Lettuce, wild lettuce, chicory, dandelion, corn salad, miner's lettuce, chickweed, common mallow, purslane, hosta shoots, New Zealand spinach, Malabar spinach, and similar greens from `I1-I3`.
- Major pest types:
  Aphids, slugs/snails, leafminers, thrips, and occasional leafhopper flare-ups.
- Likely system signals:
  This group usually fails from crop environment before it fails from one pest species. The main readings are excess nitrogen, crowding, weak airflow, hot reflected heat, or persistently wet crowns. `L3`, `L7`, `E6`, `E7`, `E8`
- Cultural practices:
  Use moderate fertility, frequent harvest, and quick turnover. Avoid long holds on overmature salad beds. Water for steady growth but shift irrigation earlier in the day. `L3`, `E6`, `E7`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Thin aggressively. Mixed-greens beds often look productive right up until they become aphid- and slug-friendly humidity traps. `L3`
- Companion and support plants:
  Edge these beds with small-flower nectar plants rather than mixing too much biomass directly into the salad row. Dill, cilantro, parsley in flower, chamomile, calendula, borage, and mountain mint are better at the border than in the leaf mass. `L3`, `L4`
- Habitat management:
  Maintain predator habitat nearby, but reduce daytime slug shelters in the bed itself. Distinguish between beneficial refuge and pest refuge. `L6`, `E7`
- Timing:
  Protect spring and fall greens with row cover at establishment if aphids or leafminers arrive early. In summer, focus more on heat, airflow, and water timing than exclusion. `E1`, `E5`, `E6`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Use row cover on new seedings, hose off aphids, remove heavily mined leaves, hand-remove slugs, and reset beds before whole sections collapse into sticky, weak growth. `E1`, `E5`, `E6`, `E7`
- Other ecological responses:
  If a mixed-greens bed repeatedly crashes, simplify the species mix and succession timing. Too many species with incompatible canopy habits can create pest shelter faster than they create resilience. `L5`

### Solanaceous fruiting crops

- Inventory coverage:
  Ground cherry and tomatillo from `I3`, plus nightshade-adjacent management implications in mixed vegetable zones.
- Major pest types:
  Flea beetles, aphids, hornworms, stink bugs, spider mites, and leafminers.
- Likely system signals:
  Early flea-beetle damage usually means the plants were exposed and slow to establish. Hornworms often track missed scouting more than failed ecology. Aphids and mites point back toward nitrogen push, drought, heat, dust, and disrupted predators. `L3`, `L7`, `E1`, `E2`, `E10`, `E11`, `E12`
- Cultural practices:
  Transplant into warm soil, mulch enough to stabilize moisture without burying stems, and avoid heavy late nitrogen. Support plants early so foliage stays off the soil and scouting remains easy. `L3`, `E1`, `E2`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Leave enough space for sun and airflow. Dense solanaceous canopies are easier for mites, hornworms, and stink bugs to hide in and harder for predators and the gardener to search. `L3`, `E12`
- Companion and support plants:
  Pair with borage, dill, cilantro, parsley, calendula, chamomile, shiso/perilla, bee balm, oregano, thyme, or sage where appropriate for nectar, pollen, and predator retention. Do not let support plants close the lower canopy. `L3`, `L4`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Preserve predator habitat, especially for minute pirate bugs, predatory stink bugs, lacewings, and spiders, but cut dust and avoid broad-spectrum cleanup that collapses natural control. `L4`, `L6`
- Timing:
  Use spring row cover on transplants until flowering where flea beetles are predictable. Remove covers when pollination is needed. Scout hornworm eggs and tiny larvae before damage becomes dramatic. `E1`, `E2`, `E10`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Handpick hornworms, crush stink bug egg masses, knock bugs into soapy water, and use strong water sprays for aphids or mites early. Clean up crop debris at season end so overwintering stink bugs and flea beetles are not sheltered in place. `E2`, `E6`, `E10`, `E11`, `E12`
- Other ecological responses:
  Kaolin clay can suppress early flea beetle feeding, and wash-downs can suppress mites, but both work best when the crop is not already under severe heat and water stress. `E2`, `E12`

### Legumes and protein crops

- Inventory coverage:
  Groundnut, Siberian pea shrub, red and white clover, common vetch, hairy vetch, crimson clover, Austrian winter pea/field pea, alfalfa, Illinois bundleflower, and ground plum from `I1-I3`.
- Major pest types:
  Aphids, leafhoppers, slugs on dense cool stands, and Mexican bean beetle where true beans enter the system later.
- Likely system signals:
  Dense nitrogen-rich mats with little airflow and weak predator access invite sap feeders. Leafhopper stress often shows up first on actively growing legumes. `L3`, `L7`, `E6`, `E8`
- Cultural practices:
  Keep legume covers and living mulches managed, not rank and lodged. Do not let companion clovers smother the crop root zone and create permanent humidity. Maintain balanced mineral support even in nitrogen-fixing plantings. `L3`, `L7`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Thin or mow support legumes before they become a mat that harbors slugs and leafhoppers. Maintain access lanes and air movement. `L3`
- Companion and support plants:
  Pair legumes with flowering herbs and composites that feed hoverflies, parasitoids, and minute pirate bugs rather than letting the legume patch serve as a monoculture insect sink. `L4`, `L5`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Low-disturbance edges help predator retention, but dense vetch tangles immediately around crop crowns can still become pest shelter. `L6`
- Timing:
  Terminate or cut back vigorous support legumes before they shade emerging crops. In annual peas or beans, row cover at establishment can prevent early aphid and beetle colonization where pressure is chronic. `E1`, `E9`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Crush aphid colonies early, rinse with water, remove badly infested bean foliage, and bag heavily infested bean residues after harvest. Where Mexican bean beetle is chronic in bean plantings, trap early adults with a sacrificial early bean patch only if it will actually be removed on time. `E6`, `E9`
- Other ecological responses:
  If the user expands into more bean production later, this row needs a true Phaseolus-focused submatrix. The current inventories are legume-heavy but not bean-heavy. `I1`, `I2`, `I3`

### Pome fruits

- Inventory coverage:
  Apple, pear, Asian pear, quince, crabapple, hawthorn, medlar, and other pome-fruit analogs from `I1`.
- Major pest types:
  Aphids, plum curculio, codling moth, mites, and mixed direct fruit feeders.
- Likely system signals:
  Repeated damage usually tracks tree architecture and sanitation failures first: canopies too tall to inspect, missed thinning, fruit left on the ground, or unmanaged neighboring hosts. `L3`, `E13`, `E14`, `E15`, `E16`
- Cultural practices:
  Choose resistant cultivars where possible and keep trees on dwarfing or otherwise reachable structure. Thin fruit well and keep the tree in annual balance rather than alternating between overcrop and lush regrowth. `E13`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Keep trees short, open, and reachable. Pruning is itself pest management here because it improves light, scouting, sanitation, bagging, and spray-free interventions. `E13`, `E15`
- Companion and support plants:
  Use flowering strips and understory plants that feed beneficials, but keep the immediate tree base clean enough that dropped fruit is easy to see and remove. Avoid letting orchard-floor vegetation hide infested fruit for weeks. `L4`, `L8`, `L9`
- Habitat management:
  Maintain beneficial habitat around the planting, but recognize that codling moth and curculio can come from outside the yard. The more neighborhood pressure there is, the more disciplined sanitation and exclusion need to be. `L4`, `E14`, `E15`
- Timing:
  Hang pheromone traps early in spring for codling moth monitoring. Remove infested fruit beginning about six to eight weeks after bloom and keep doing it. Bag fruit four to six weeks after bloom when fruit is roughly one-half to one inch across. `E14`, `E15`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Pick up dropped fruit all season, destroy infested fruit, inspect storage areas and crates for cocoons, and use pheromone traps to see whether moth pressure is real. Fruit bagging is the strongest nonchemical exclusion move for small trees. `E14`, `E15`
- Other ecological responses:
  Curculio pressure is often worse near woods, fencerows, and trashy edges. If that is the site condition, treat sanitation and monitoring as mandatory, not optional. `E16`

### Stone fruits

- Inventory coverage:
  Peach/nectarine, plum, apricot, sweet cherry, sour cherry, Chickasaw plum, American plum, and related Prunus crops in `I1`.
- Major pest types:
  Plum curculio, oriental fruit moth, peach tree borer, spotted wing drosophila on susceptible cherries, and generalist sap feeders/stink bugs.
- Likely system signals:
  Heavy pressure is amplified by dense unpruned trees, drought stress, fruit left on the ground, and delayed harvest on susceptible fruit. `L3`, `E16`, `E17`, `E19`
- Cultural practices:
  Keep the tree in balance with annual pruning and fruit thinning. Prevent drought stress, especially during fruit swell. Do not let fruit mummies or dropped fruit accumulate. `E17`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Open-center training is part of pest and disease suppression because it maximizes airflow and light penetration and keeps fruit reachable for scouting and removal. `E17`
- Companion and support plants:
  Use flowering borders and low understories for beneficials, but avoid tall unmanaged vegetation that makes dropped fruit invisible or keeps trunks damp. `L4`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Favor beneficial habitat near but not inside the trunk flare and scaffold zone. Tree-borer and fruit-feeder systems do better with a visible base, low weed competition, and open monitoring lines. `L4`, `E17`
- Timing:
  Monitor from bloom through petal fall and especially through the post-petal-fall period when curculio activity ramps. Harvest cherries and other soft fruit promptly once they become susceptible to SWD. `E16`, `E19`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Remove dropped or visibly damaged fruit fast. Protect trunks from borers with good tree vigor and monitoring at the crown. Netting or bagging can be justified for small trees with repeated direct fruit attack. `E17`, `E19`
- Other ecological responses:
  Stone fruit is less forgiving than pome fruit. If ecological management is the goal, keep planting size modest enough that pruning, sanitation, and harvest timing remain realistic. `E17`

### Soft berries and shrub fruits

- Inventory coverage:
  Raspberry, blackberry, black raspberry, blueberry, elderberry, currant, gooseberry, honeyberry/haskap, serviceberry, chokeberry, and related soft-fruit shrubs from `I1`.
- Major pest types:
  Spotted wing drosophila on ripening soft fruit, Japanese beetles, cane borers in brambles, aphids, mites, and plant bugs or sap beetles where fruit is held too long.
- Likely system signals:
  The biggest signals are overripe fruit, old canes left standing, dense humid interiors, wild brambles too close to the planting, and irregular harvest. `L3`, `E18`, `E19`, `E24`
- Cultural practices:
  Harvest frequently and cool fruit quickly. Renovate brambles every year. Choose sites with good sun and enough afternoon relief where the crop benefits from it without collapsing airflow. `E18`, `E23`, `E24`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Keep raspberry rows narrow, blackberry canes thinned, and shrub interiors open. UMD specifically recommends annual renovation pruning and keeping red raspberry rows under about 18 inches wide. `E18`
- Companion and support plants:
  Use flower strips or insectary edges nearby, but do not let them merge into the berry canopy. Berry plantings need distinct picking lanes and dry interiors more than they need maximal vegetative abundance. `L4`, `L8`, `L9`
- Habitat management:
  Nearby beneficial habitat helps, but berry systems also need rigorous edge management. Remove invasive wineberry and keep wild Rubus from sitting right on top of the planting. `E18`
- Timing:
  Pick berries often enough that fruit does not sit at peak susceptibility. For SWD-prone crops, begin close monitoring as fruit colors and softens. `E18`, `E19`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Remove cull fruit, prune out spent floricanes to ground level, remove pruning waste, and protect high-value bushes with netting or fine mesh where fruit flies or birds are chronic. `E18`, `E19`, `E24`
- Other ecological responses:
  Currants and gooseberries generally have lighter insect pressure than brambles, but they still need pruning, airflow, and sanitation. Do not let "minor insect concern" become "no management needed." `E24`

### Grapes, kiwis, and other fruiting vines

- Inventory coverage:
  European grape, fox grape/labrusca grape, muscadine, hardy kiwi, arctic kiwi, and maypop from `I1`.
- Major pest types:
  Japanese beetles, grape berry moth, phylloxera/root-feeding aphid complexes, spotted lanternfly, and spotted wing drosophila on overripe kiwiberry fruit.
- Likely system signals:
  Unmanaged wild grape on a wooded edge amplifies berry-moth pressure. Dense unpruned canopy amplifies humidity and makes clusters hard to inspect. Overripe fruit left hanging or dropped invites SWD in kiwiberry. `L3`, `E20`, `E21`, `E22`, `E25`
- Cultural practices:
  Choose resistant or appropriate rootstocks and American-type grape material where phylloxera pressure matters. Keep vines trained and renewed so clusters are visible and reachable. `E20`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Prune hard enough to keep light and air in the fruiting zone. Berry moth, rot, and general scouting all get worse in tangled canopy. `E20`, `E21`
- Companion and support plants:
  Flower strips can support beneficials around vineyards and vines, but they should not shade the fruit zone or prevent access for scouting clustered fruit. `L4`, `L8`, `L9`
- Habitat management:
  Watch the wooded border and nearby wild grape patches, because UMD notes grape berry moth pressure is often concentrated nearest the woods. Eliminate nearby tree-of-heaven where feasible because it concentrates SLF. `E21`, `E22`
- Timing:
  Knock Japanese beetles off in early July while adults are concentrated. Watch for berry-moth symptoms and cluster webbing in midseason. Pick kiwiberries clean and remove drops. `E20`, `E21`, `E25`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Hand-remove damaged berries in light infestations, knock Japanese beetles into soapy water, and use circle traps for SLF on suitable trunks rather than blanket yard sprays. `E20`, `E21`, `E22`
- Other ecological responses:
  At garden scale, hand removal plus canopy opening and cluster scouting is usually the simpler ecological response for Japanese beetles than building a trap-based strategy around the vine row. `E20`

### Specialty lower-pressure fruits and perennials

- Inventory coverage:
  Pawpaw, persimmon, fig, mulberry, jujube, serviceberry, chokeberry, elderberry, tea plant, spicebush, and other specialty woody crops from `I1`.
- Major pest types:
  Often lower insect pressure than apples or peaches, but not no pressure: sap feeders, Japanese beetles on some hosts, spotted lanternfly on favored woody plants, mites, and fruit-fly pressure when fruit is allowed to overripen.
- Likely system signals:
  The main failures here are usually site and management errors: drought, wrong light level, crowding, missed harvest, or assuming native/adapted plants can be ignored. `L1`, `L3`, `E22`, `E23`
- Cultural practices:
  Give each species the site it actually wants. Keep trees and shrubs vigorous but not overfed. Protect trunks from mechanical and wildlife injury, because bark wounds become bigger problems than insect feeding on many of these crops. `E23`
- Spacing and canopy:
  Maintain open structure and remove weak or crossing wood so fruit can be inspected and harvested on time. `E23`
- Companion and support plants:
  These crops fit well into insectary-rich edges and polycultures, but do not let support species eliminate visibility at the plant base or harvest access to the fruit. `L4`, `L8`
- Habitat management:
  Native-fruit edges can be excellent beneficial habitat. Keep that value while still managing fallen fruit, suckers, and crowding. `L4`, `L9`, `E23`
- Timing:
  Harvest promptly once ripe. Pawpaw in particular often suffers more from fallen-fruit losses and wildlife than from actionable insect feeding, while kiwiberry-like specialty fruit can pick up fly pressure if left hanging too long. `E23`, `E25`
- Sanitation, trapping, and exclusion:
  Pick up drops, protect trunks if wildlife rubbing or chewing is likely, and use targeted SLF trapping where relevant instead of broad insecticidal responses. `E22`, `E23`
- Other ecological responses:
  UMD notes pawpaw foliage has few insect feeders that justify treatment. The ecological lesson is to keep this group low-input, but not unmanaged. `E23`
