# Interconnections

This file exists because isolated answers are usually wrong or incomplete in ecological agriculture.

## Default Cross-Topic Checks

Before answering, test these six connection axes:

1. Climate and seasonal timing
2. Soil organic matter, nutrient balance, and biological activity
3. Spatial structure: canopy, spacing, layering, or plant mixtures
4. Pest pressure, beneficial habitat, and disturbance regime
5. Succession timing, labor bottlenecks, and harvest windows
6. Perennial / wild integration opportunities

If any axis materially changes the answer, include it.

## Strongest Topic Bridges

### `01` <-> `02`

Microfarm logic becomes real only through bed turnover, labor, market fit, and crop sequencing.

### `02` <-> `10`

Succession and interplanting cannot be separated. Plant mixtures change canopy closure, bed occupancy, harvest timing, and weed or pest dynamics.

### `02` <-> `12`

Intensive annual beds only work ecologically if beneficials can persist and if the pest load is read as a system signal, not just an enemy list.

### `05` <-> `06` <-> `07`

Bulk organic matter, active biology, and fungal or rhizosphere effects are different layers of the same soil-health question. Do not collapse them into one thing, but do not treat them as unrelated.

### `03` <-> `05`

Biochar is most defensible when connected to composting, nutrient capture, and soil-structure strategy rather than treated as a magic standalone input.

### `04` <-> `05` <-> `06`

Bokashi and EM-style management sit between raw fertility handling and active biological management. They should be judged against composting goals, microbial claims, cost, and actual management burden.

### `07` <-> `12`

Plant defense is not only about predators and parasitoids. Mycorrhizae, rhizosphere biology, and induced resistance can change whether pest pressure takes hold in the first place.

### `08` <-> `11`

Forest gardens and wild edible integration overlap whenever useful wild species are tolerated, transplanted, or designed into managed perennial systems.

### `08` <-> `12`

Perennial habitat structure changes overwintering sites, flowering continuity, trophic complexity, and therefore pest-control potential.

### `09` <-> all other topics

Most ecological methods are climate- and timing-sensitive. Add Mid-Atlantic reality checks to anything that involves sowing dates, overwintering, disease pressure, decomposition, or beneficial habitat persistence.

## Pest Pressure As Information

Do not reduce pest questions to product choice or companion-plant folklore.

Check whether the outbreak is more plausibly signaling:

- soluble nitrogen excess
- weak mineral balance
- drought or moisture swings
- stalled growth and poor establishment
- low soil-biological buffering
- lack of flowering continuity for beneficials
- over-disturbance from tillage, cleanup, or spray habit
- over-simple crop structure

The answer should say which of these seem most likely and why.

## Garden-Planning Rule

A garden plan is incomplete unless it covers all of these layers:

- crop placement
- succession timing
- fertility and residue handling
- beneficial habitat
- pest monitoring
- harvest timing
- next-bed transition

## Interview-Mode Rule

When building a weekly timeline, do not track only sowing and harvest. Also track:

- compost and residue flows
- inoculation or biological actions when relevant
- flower-strip or insectary maintenance
- trap crop setup or removal
- monitoring windows for expected pests and diseases
- succession decisions before the previous crop is fully done
